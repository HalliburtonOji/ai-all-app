## Where AI is genuinely useful in this job

- **Drafts.** Always drafts. Almost any kind of draft — long-form, social, emails, briefs. Editing AI output is faster than starting empty.
- **Research compression.** Paste a long article + ask for the 5 most important points. Or "what would an opposing view say?" — surfaces the gaps in your own thinking.
- **Voice alignment.** Paste 3 examples of your client's voice + ask the model to mimic it for a fresh piece. Way better than "make it sound professional."
- **The boring middle.** Bullet-point notes → full paragraphs. The mental friction of "now I have to make these complete sentences" is exactly what AI is good at.
- **Editing passes.** "Make this 30% shorter without losing X." "Find the three weakest sentences." "Where am I burying the lede?"

## Where AI is not the right tool

- **Original reporting.** Conversations with sources, on-the-ground observations, interviews. AI can transcribe and summarise after; it can't go.
- **The actual point of view.** What you *think* about the topic, the angle nobody else has — that's the reason anyone reads you. Don't outsource it.
- **Anything where being wrong is expensive.** A confident hallucinated stat in a corporate piece will outlive the piece. Verify everything load-bearing.
- **Tone you don't already have.** AI mimics styles it has seen. If you want something genuinely new, you have to write it.

## Three indispensable moves

1. **Become the editor.** Most pros now generate first drafts faster than they can manually write. The job has shifted: more time on shaping, less on producing. Practice ruthless editing — your value compounds there.
2. **Develop a perspective worth paying for.** Generic-good writing is now free. What's not free: a clear, repeated angle on a topic that matters. Build one.
3. **Build trust with named sources.** When AI commodifies generic writing, what's scarce is *trustworthy* writing. Cite real people, real numbers, real on-the-record sources. The premium for that just went up.

## Smart defaults

- Use the Studio text drafter here for the first 70% of any piece you'd write the same way.
- Disclose-AI when the piece pretends to be your raw thinking — readers feel deceived if they later notice.
- Charge for editing time, not word count, when AI is in the loop. Word count is no longer a useful proxy for effort.

## What to avoid

- Defending generic-good writing. You won't win that fight — and the people doing it are not your peers anymore.
- Pretending AI isn't in your workflow. It is in your competitors'. Be honest with yourself first.
- Letting your voice flatten toward AI-default prose. If you read your stuff and can't tell if you wrote it or the model did, that's a flag.
