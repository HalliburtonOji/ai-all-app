## Why this matters

Most career advice about AI is either "you'll be replaced" doom or "AI will 10x you" hype. Both are useless for planning. The grounded version: some skills compound, some atrophy, and the gap between them widens every year. Knowing which is which lets you invest your hours where they actually pay off.

## Skills that compound over time

These get more valuable as AI gets more capable — because they're the things AI doesn't do, or can't yet do well.

### 1. Taste

The ability to look at three options and pick the right one for the audience and context. AI generates volume; humans pick what's worth shipping. As volume goes up, taste's price tag goes up.

How to build it: consume a lot of work in your field, deliberately. Note what you'd change in everything you read or see. Develop opinions and defend them in writing.

### 2. Real-world context

What you know about your specific industry, geography, audience, history. AI knows what's in its training data. Your private context — clients, customer conversations, market quirks, regulatory edges — is yours.

How to build it: stay close to actual users / clients / readers. Take the calls. Visit the place. Don't outsource the listening.

### 3. Owning end-to-end outcomes

Saying "I'll handle this and tell you when it's done" is a different job from "I'll do my part." Outcome ownership requires judgment, sequencing, accountability, and stakeholder management — none of which AI does for you.

How to build it: take on one project where you own the result, not just the pieces. Learn from every mismatched expectation along the way.

### 4. Trusted relationships

Repeat buyers. Referrals. People who pick you over the option of just using AI directly. Trust is the unfakeable thing.

How to build it: deliver. Communicate proactively. Be honest when you've messed up. Show up over years, not weeks.

### 5. A specific point of view

What you uniquely think about your domain. The angle nobody else has, that you can articulate publicly and consistently. AI averages; you specialise.

How to build it: write publicly. Take positions. Be willing to be wrong in print. The reps compound fast.

## Skills that atrophy without effort

These don't disappear, but their wage curve gets flatter. They were once differentiators; now they're table stakes.

- **Pure production volume.** Drafting fast was once a moat. Now everyone drafts fast.
- **Generic technical knowledge.** "I can build a CRUD app" / "I can write blog posts" / "I can edit videos." All real, all available cheap.
- **Toolchain memorisation.** The Adobe shortcuts, the Excel function library, the framework idioms. AI fills these gaps.
- **Following best practices to the letter.** Best practices are now democratised. Distinctiveness wins more than compliance.

You don't have to "give these up" — they're still useful. Just don't expect them alone to anchor your career five years out.

## What to deprioritise without panicking

- Becoming an "AI prompt engineer" as your primary identity. The category is shrinking, not growing.
- Chasing every new AI tool that ships.
- "AI-first" rebrand of your existing skill — buyers don't care about the workflow, they care about the work.
- Selling courses about "How to use AI" unless you have actual receipts of running a real business with it.

## The strange truth

The skills that compound are mostly the same skills that always compounded — taste, context, trust, ownership, perspective — but now without the camouflage of routine production work that used to fill the days.

You'll have more time for the compounding skills, *if* you spend it on them. Most people will spend the freed-up time chasing new tools or doing more of the now-cheap work. The ones who shift toward the compounding skills will do remarkably well.

## Try it

Spend 20 minutes today on one of the compounding skills:

- Watch a piece of work in your field and write 200 words on what you'd change.
- Email three former clients with a sharp follow-up question — not a sales note.
- Write 300 words taking a specific position on something in your industry. Post it (or save it for later).

Twenty minutes a day, every day, for the next five years. That's the long game.
