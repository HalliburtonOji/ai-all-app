## Why this matters

Three years ago, "I have a clean portfolio of professional work" was a real edge. Now it's table stakes — anyone with reasonable taste can produce one. The buyers (clients, hiring managers, audiences) have noticed. What you put in your portfolio in 2026 is different from what you put in 2022.

## What buyers actually look for now

When the work itself is partially AI-assistable, buyers up-shift to evaluating *you*. They look for:

1. **A point of view.** Why this approach, not the obvious-AI-default? What did you reject?
2. **Specifics about the brief.** Real client + real constraint + real outcome. Not lorem-ipsum case studies.
3. **A "before and after" story.** What was wrong with version 1 and how did your judgment fix it?
4. **Evidence of taste.** What you noticed that an AI didn't catch. The polish call.
5. **Repeat clients.** That you keep someone happy is more meaningful than how shiny one piece looks.

## What to deemphasise

- **Volume.** Twenty pieces with no story is worse than five with stories.
- **Generic deliverables.** Logos with no client context, generic copy decks, "various social posts." These now read as AI-or-junior work, even when they're not.
- **Aspirational mood boards.** Anyone can paste a Pinterest grid. The bar is now real evidence of execution.
- **"AI-powered" framing.** Your audience doesn't care about your stack. They care whether you delivered.

## What to add

### Process notes alongside output

A 60-word note for each piece saying: what the brief was, what you tried first, what you scrapped, what the final call was. This is the layer AI can't fake.

### A "decision log"

Short bullets on a few pieces saying what you specifically chose and why. "Picked muted palette over the obvious option because the client's existing site was already very saturated." That's senior-judgment evidence in 18 words.

### Failure stories

A small section. "This pitch I sent never got a reply. Here's what I think I'd change." Counter-intuitively, this builds more credibility than a wins-only portfolio. Most pros don't do it. That's an opportunity.

### Disclose-AI on relevant pieces

For pieces where AI was meaningfully in the loop, a small note: "First-draft AI-assisted; final shape and 70% of copy is me." Positions you as honest + skilled, not as gatekeeper or apologist.

## The portfolio passport pattern

This app has a portfolio passport at `/p/[username]` — public if you opt in. Same pattern works on any portfolio site. Treat it as a *living document*: update one piece every two weeks. Buyers can tell when a portfolio is current vs. frozen in 2023.

## What this looks like in practice

A designer's "best of" page in 2022: 12 polished images.

A designer's "best of" page in 2026: 5 polished images, each with a 3-sentence story about the brief, one quote from a client (with permission), and a "what I'd do differently" line on the messiest one.

The 2026 version converts better. It always has, but the gap got wider.

## What to avoid

- **Padding.** Better to have 5 strong pieces than 15 weak ones.
- **Hiding the constraints.** "Done in 2 weeks for £400" is more impressive than the same work shown without context.
- **Performing for the algorithm.** Portfolios that look like Behance bait age fast.

## Try it

Open your current portfolio (or a doc where you'd start one). Pick the 3 pieces you'd keep if you could keep only 3. Write a 60-word process note for each. Notice what comes alive that wasn't there before.
