## Why this matters

There's a growing readership that resents finding out work was AI-drafted. There's also a growing reader fatigue around hyper-defensive "as an AI…" disclaimers on everything. The wholesome charter of this app is built around getting this right: you keep your audience's trust by being honest about how the work was made, when it matters.

## The simple rule

Disclose when **it would change how the reader interprets the work** if they knew.

Reader trust is downstream of accurate expectations. Mismatch → broken trust.

## When disclosure matters

- **The piece is sold as your original thinking.** Bylined essay, a paid newsletter, an opinion column. If a meaningful chunk was AI-drafted, the buyer thought they were buying you. Tell them.
- **Citations or numbers are present.** AI hallucinates citations confidently. If you didn't verify each one, say so or remove them.
- **The reader is making a decision based on the content.** Medical info, legal info, financial info. Stakes are high; AI confidence is not a guarantee.
- **It's a personal communication that pretends to be hand-written.** A condolence note, a hiring rejection, a thank-you. If it's AI-drafted, the recipient feels different reading it than they would have. They get to know.
- **Code that goes into production.** Add a comment. Future-you reading the diff in 3 months will thank you.

## When disclosure is overkill

- **AI helped you brainstorm but you wrote it.** That's just a tool, like a notebook.
- **You used AI to fix typos or tighten grammar.** Spellcheck doesn't need disclosure either.
- **It's a routine email or message in your normal voice.** "Hey, can we move tomorrow's call to 3pm?" — the world doesn't need a disclosure on this.
- **AI summarised a public document for your own consumption.** Internal tool use; no audience.

## How to disclose

Three lightweight options, in order of formality:

1. **A footer line.** *"Drafted with AI; edited and verified by me."* One line at the bottom. Doesn't get in the way.
2. **An inline note.** *"This was drafted with AI as a starting point. I rewrote the conclusion myself."* When the reader cares about the difference.
3. **The "Disclose-AI" tag.** A short standard label some communities are starting to use. Recognisable to readers in those communities.

## What never works

- **"I would never use AI for this."** When the truth is you did. Don't.
- **Burying the disclosure.** Footnote in 8pt grey text. Reader feels deceived.
- **Over-disclosing on routine work.** Disclaimers on every email feel performative and dilute the signal.

## What this protects

- Your reputation when people figure out (they sometimes do).
- The reader's right to interpret the work accurately.
- The category overall — when AI is everywhere, the people who are clear about their workflow earn trust the dishonest ones lose.

## Try it

Pick the AI-assisted thing you'll ship next. Decide *now* whether disclosure matters for it (using the rule above). Write down your reasoning in one sentence. The act of deciding deliberately makes you a better practitioner.
