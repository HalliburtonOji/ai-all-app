## Why this matters

The single biggest gap between people getting useful AI output and people getting generic slop is *the prompt itself*. The model can't read your mind. The clearer the ask, the better the answer — by a lot.

## The shape of a clear prompt

A clear prompt usually has four parts, in some order:

1. **Who you are or who's reading.** "I'm a freelance designer pitching a small clinic." "The audience is parents of young kids."
2. **What you actually want.** Not "help with my email" — "draft a 3-paragraph email to a client explaining a 2-week delay, apologetic but not grovelling".
3. **The constraints.** Word count, tone, format, things to avoid.
4. **The starting material.** The text to edit, the bullet points to expand, the screenshot to summarise. Paste it.

Most prompts that fail fail because part 2, 3, or 4 was skipped.

## Vague vs specific — same model, different planet

Vague:

> Help me write a tweet about my product.

What this gets you: a generic, hashtag-laden tweet that nobody on your timeline would post.

Specific:

> I run a freelance video editing service for indie YouTubers. Write a tweet (≤200 chars, no hashtags, no emoji) that would make a creator with 10k–50k subs DM me to ask about pricing. Tone: confident, slightly understated, not salesy. Avoid the words "elevate", "unlock", "transform".

What this gets you: a tweet you might actually post.

## Politeness is irrelevant

You don't need "please". You don't need "if it's not too much trouble". You don't need "I appreciate your help". The model is not insulted by directness. It's not flattered by warmth. Use whatever tone makes *you* think more clearly. Most pros write prompts that read like terse instructions to a sharp colleague.

## What to avoid

- **"Make it good."** Define good. Define for whom. Define against what.
- **"Be creative."** Creative how? Surrealist? Scrappy? The model will guess and probably guess wrong.
- **One vague ask after another.** If the first reply isn't right, the fix is usually "I should have said more in prompt 1," not "let me argue with the model in prompt 2."

## Try it

Pick something you've recently asked AI in a one-line prompt. Rewrite it using the four-part structure above. Send both versions to the coach in this Project and compare what comes back.
