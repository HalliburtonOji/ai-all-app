## Why this matters

Most "prompt libraries" online are useless because they pretend a magic incantation will fix any problem. It won't. But three or four well-shaped templates that you actually use? That's a real productivity lever.

## Template 1 — Draft a thing for an audience

```
You're a [specific role].
The audience is [specific audience].
The format is [thing — email, tweet, paragraph, scene, etc.].

Goal: [what you want the reader to think, feel, or do].

Constraints:
- Length: [number]
- Tone: [adjectives, ideally with one example]
- Avoid: [the words / framings / clichés you don't want]

Here's [the input — bullet points, raw notes, the question, the
context]:

<your stuff>
```

Use this when: you need a draft of anything written.

## Template 2 — Critique my work

```
You're a [specific reviewer]. I'm asking you to be honest, not nice.
The piece is for [audience], aiming to [goal].

Read this:

<your draft>

Then tell me:
1. What's working
2. What's weakest
3. The single change that would lift it most

Be terse. No "great job" preambles.
```

Use this when: you have a draft and want a real second look. Specific reviewer matters — "a freelance editor who works on B2B newsletters" is way better than "an editor."

## Template 3 — Decision-support, not advice

```
You're a [role]. I have a decision to make and I want you to map the
trade-off, not pick for me.

The decision: [what you're choosing between].

What I know about my situation:
- [fact]
- [fact]
- [constraint]

What I don't know that might matter:
- [open question]

Output:
1. The 2-3 core factors I should be weighing.
2. For each option, the strongest case for it (in one paragraph).
3. The single question that, if I answered honestly, would probably
   tip my choice.

Don't recommend. Lay out the chessboard.
```

Use this when: you're tempted to ask AI "what should I do?" and you don't actually want it to decide for you.

## How to use these

1. Save them in a notes app, or paste them into a Project's coach as starting points.
2. Tweak the slots — don't change the shape.
3. After 5–10 uses, you'll naturally adapt them to your style. That's good.

## What templates can't fix

- A vague goal. If you don't know what you want, no template will save you.
- Bad inputs. Garbage in, garbage out — the templates assume you've thought about the inputs.
- A decision that needs you. AI can map a chessboard. Only you can move.

## Try it

Pick the template that fits a real task you have today. Use it. Save the version that worked for you somewhere persistent. That's now yours.
