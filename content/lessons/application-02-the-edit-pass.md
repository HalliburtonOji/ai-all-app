## Why this matters

Almost everyone who uses AI poorly skips the edit. They read the draft, think "that's pretty good," and ship. Almost everyone who uses AI well treats the draft as the *starting* point, not the ending.

The edit pass is where your taste, your context, your judgment — the things AI doesn't have — get baked into the work.

## The 4 moves of a good edit pass

### 1. Cut the throat-clearing

AI drafts often open with a generic preamble: "In today's fast-paced world…" "It's important to note that…" "When considering X, there are several factors…"

Delete those sentences. The reader wants the substance, not the windup. About 15% of any AI draft is throat-clearing.

### 2. Replace the generic phrases

AI defaults to safe-generic language. Look for:

- "leverage" → "use"
- "utilise" → "use"
- "in order to" → "to"
- "due to the fact that" → "because"
- "unleash your potential" / "unlock value" / "transform your X" → just delete
- "It's important to remember that..." → just delete

You'd never write these unprompted. Your reader notices when they're there.

### 3. Add specifics where it generalised

Find the paragraphs that could apply to anyone, anywhere. Make them yours:

- Generic: "Many businesses face challenges with customer engagement."
- Yours: "I had three clients last quarter ghost me after the first call."

Specifics beat abstractions in every kind of writing — newsletters, emails, code comments, sales copy. AI generalises by default; you de-generalise.

### 4. Read it aloud

Three minutes. You'll catch:

- The sentence that's too long.
- The phrasing that sounds slightly stilted.
- The transition that doesn't quite land.
- The bit that's just wrong.

If you can't read it without stumbling, neither can your reader.

## What this looks like in practice

For a 200-word AI-drafted email:

- 30 seconds: cut throat-clearing
- 1 minute: replace generic phrases with my voice
- 1 minute: add 1-2 specific details
- 30 seconds: read aloud

That's 3 minutes per real-work draft. Across a week of AI-assisted work that's maybe an hour. You're paying that hour to make sure your work doesn't sound like everyone else's AI-drafted work.

## The trap to avoid

Some people use AI to write their first draft, then ask AI to edit it. Don't. Editing is where YOUR taste enters the work. If you outsource that too, you've shipped pure-AI output with extra steps.

It's fine to ask AI to *suggest* edits. It's not fine to let AI *make* the final call.

## Try it

Take an AI-drafted thing you produced in the last 7 days (or draft one now). Run the 4-move edit pass on it. Notice what changed. That's your taste, on the page.
