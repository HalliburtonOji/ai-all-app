## Why this matters

Reaching for AI on every task is a beginner move. The moment you've internalised when AI helps and when it gets in the way, you stop wasting cycles and start producing better work. This is what tool fluency actually looks like.

## When to skip AI entirely

- **Tiny tasks.** "Two-line email to my mum." Just write it. The cost of summoning a tool is higher than just doing it.
- **You already know what you're doing.** A skilled writer drafting in their flow doesn't need AI. They might use it for editing, but the actual writing is theirs. Same for a senior engineer in the middle of a feature they've thought through.
- **Live human conversation.** Sales calls, interviews, difficult conversations with people. AI is not a co-pilot here. Your human judgment is.
- **Anything where being on autopilot is dangerous.** Driving. Surgery. Operating heavy machinery. (Less obvious: parenting decisions, financial decisions about your own life, important emails to people you care about.)
- **When you should be thinking, not producing.** AI is good at producing text. But sometimes the right move is to stare out the window for 20 minutes.

## When to use it sparingly

- **The first 30 seconds of a creative task.** Many writers will tell you the empty page is the best teacher. If you AI your way past the discomfort, you might skip past the thinking that would have made the work distinctive.
- **Anything that becomes part of your identity.** You can use AI to draft a first wedding speech, but you should rewrite every line in your own voice. Otherwise it's not yours.
- **Critical thinking exercises.** "Should I take this job?" — let AI help you map the chessboard, but don't let it pick a square for you.

## The "AI in the loop, not at the wheel" rule

A useful frame: AI is the assistant, you're the principal. The assistant drafts, suggests, transforms, lists. The principal decides, edits, signs, ships.

Reverse that and you'll produce a lot of work that nobody — including you — actually owns.

## What this looks like in practice

- Coach drafts your pitch → **you** rewrite the opening line in your own voice → ship.
- Studio image gen produces 8 candidate thumbnails → **you** pick one and edit it manually → ship.
- AI summarises a 40-page doc → **you** verify the 3 claims that matter most → make a call.

## Try it

This week, pick one task you'd normally reach for AI on, and do it without. Notice what's harder. Notice what's easier. Notice what comes out different. The point isn't anti-AI moralising — it's keeping your own muscles working.
