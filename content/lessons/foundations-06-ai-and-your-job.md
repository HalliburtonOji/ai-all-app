## Why this matters

Every day, someone with something to sell tells you AI will either save your job or destroy it. Both are usually nonsense. The truth is messier and more useful: it depends on the job, the person, and what you do about it.

## What AI is genuinely good at

- **Drafting.** First versions of emails, reports, code, copy, scripts.
- **Summarising and transforming.** Turning long things into short things, formats into other formats, messes into structure.
- **Pattern-matching.** Finding the typical answer to a typical problem.
- **Tireless small judgment.** Filling out the boring middle of a task you already understand.

Most jobs have a lot of this kind of work, which is why AI is showing up everywhere.

## What AI is genuinely bad at

- **Owning a non-trivial outcome end-to-end.** It will start strong and lose the plot somewhere in the middle.
- **Knowing what's actually true** in your specific context — your client, your codebase, your market, this week.
- **Judgment calls under uncertainty.** Especially when the stakes matter, or when there's no "typical" answer.
- **Being held accountable.** Models don't get fired. People do. Which means people still have to own the work.

## What this means for most jobs

The honest middle answer:

- **A lot of routine work shrinks.** Things that were 4 hours become 30 minutes.
- **Senior judgment becomes more valuable.** Knowing which AI output is good, which is wrong, which is dangerous — that taste is the new differentiator.
- **The bar for output rises.** If everyone can draft a passable thing, the people who can ship a sharp, distinctive thing pull further ahead.
- **Some specific roles do shrink.** Repetitive content production, low-end customer support tiers, simple document review, basic transcription. Often slowly, often with re-shuffling rather than mass cuts.

## What's actually worth doing

- **Use it on your own work.** Not theoretically — actually. The fastest learning is via the jobs you already do.
- **Get good at the verification side.** Know how to spot a hallucination, an off-tone draft, a confidently wrong recommendation.
- **Document your AI workflow.** When colleagues ask "how did you do that?", a clear story is gold.
- **Don't trust your own enthusiasm.** Pause before publishing anything AI-generated to a real audience. The wholesome charter (this app's spine) holds — Disclose-AI when it matters.

## What's not worth doing

- Doomscrolling AI takes.
- Buying every AI tool that ships.
- Writing your CV around "AI experience" if you don't actually have any yet.
- Catastrophising about your specific job before you've checked what AI can and can't do for it.

## Try it

Pick one task you do every week at your job (or in your side project). Spend 30 minutes attempting it with the coach in this Project. Then write yourself one line: *what was useful, what was not, what I'd do differently next time.* Repeat across a few tasks. Patterns will emerge.
