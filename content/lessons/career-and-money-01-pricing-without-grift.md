## Why this matters

There's a lot of bad pricing advice out there about AI work. "Charge for the result, not the time." "AI lets you 10x your rates." "Your clients don't need to know you're using AI." Every one of these is partially true and largely wrong, and they all skip past the question that actually matters: what's a fair price for what you delivered?

## The honest framing

What you're actually selling is some combination of:

1. **The output** — the deliverable in the client's hand.
2. **The judgment** — knowing what good looks like, what to push back on, what to skip.
3. **The accountability** — when something goes wrong, you fix it. The model doesn't take the call.
4. **The trust** — they'd pick you again over any model that's competing for the same work.

AI changes 1 (output is faster). It barely changes 2-4. So your price reflects the proportion of value coming from each.

## A working pricing mental model

Old pricing logic: hours × rate.
New pricing logic: **(deliverable scope × your judgment value) + accountability premium**.

The "judgment value" is real. The accountability premium is real. The "AI typed faster than I would have" is not a discount you owe the client; it's productivity you earned by getting good with the tools.

But — there's a real-world ceiling. The client knows AI exists. They've used Claude themselves. If your price assumes they don't know what AI can do, they'll quietly find someone who priced for the world they actually live in.

## When to charge MORE than your old rate

- The work is high-stakes. (Legal-ish, financial, brand-defining.)
- You're owning a specific outcome they can't get from raw AI.
- You bring specialised judgment they can't easily evaluate or replicate.

## When to charge LESS than your old rate

- The deliverable is genuinely faster *and* simpler than it used to be (and the client knows it).
- You're competing with people who can do the same thing for less, and your differentiation isn't there yet.
- It's volume-shaped work where the per-unit margin can shrink because the unit time shrunk.

## When to charge the same

The most common honest answer. Your rate reflects judgment + accountability + relationship. AI didn't change those. You're just delivering the same value with less drudgery on your side.

## The conversation with the client

Don't pretend AI isn't in your workflow. Don't lead with "I use AI for everything" either.

A reasonable framing:

> "I use AI tools in my workflow where they help — usually for first drafts and routine production. Final shape, decisions, and the actual quality call are still me. My rate reflects that."

Most clients respect that more than either extreme.

## What to avoid

- **"AI doesn't matter, my rate is my rate."** Ostrich pose. Doesn't work for long.
- **"AI changes everything, I should 10x my prices."** It doesn't. You'll lose clients who run the math.
- **Hiding AI use in negotiations.** When they find out (they sometimes do), you've lost the relationship.
- **Devaluing yourself because "AI did the work."** The client paid for the result, not the typing speed.

## Try it

Write down the three rates you'd quote today for: a quick task (~30 min), a half-day deliverable, and a multi-week project. For each, name what the client is paying for that AI couldn't replace. If you can't name it, your pricing isn't ready yet — fix that before quoting.
